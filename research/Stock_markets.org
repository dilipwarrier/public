* Stock market performance                                           :Review:

In the 40 years leading up to 2021, global stocks had an average annual
return of 7.4% and global bonds 6.3%. However, the long-run averages
for those numbers are 5% and 1.7% a year respectively.

Youngsters investing after 2021 should not expect the returns of the past
40 years. Those returns have been extraordinary.

* The Sharpe ratio

Consider \(n\) risky assets with random return vector \(R \in \mathbb{R}^n\).

Let
\[
\mu := \mathbb{E}[R] \in \mathbb{R}^n,
\qquad
\Sigma := \mathrm{Cov}(R) \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}.
\]

Assume a risk-free asset exists with a return \(r_f\).
Let \(\mathbf{1}\) denote the all-ones vector.

A portfolio of risky assets is represented by a weight vector \(w\).
If the investor allocates only among risky assets, a common constraint is
\[
\mathbf{1}^\top w = 1.
\]

Portfolio mean and variance:
\[
\mu_p(w) = w^\top \mu,
\qquad
\sigma_p^2(w) = w^\top \Sigma w,
\qquad
\sigma_p(w) = \sqrt{w^\top \Sigma w}.
\]

Define the excess-return (risk premium) vector:
\[
z := \mu - r_f \mathbf{1}.
\]

Then the portfolio excess mean is
\[
\mu_p(w) - r_f = w^\top z.
\]

The Sharpe ratio of the portfolio \(w\) is
\[
S(w) := \frac{\mu_p(w)-r_f}{\sigma_p(w)}
= \frac{w^\top z}{\sqrt{w^\top \Sigma w}},
\]
defined for \(w\) such that \(w^\top \Sigma w > 0\).

Because of scale invariance, one standard approach is:
\[
\max_{w \neq 0} \frac{(w^\top z)^2}{w^\top \Sigma w}
\quad \Longleftrightarrow \quad
\max_{w} (w^\top z) \ \text{s.t.}\ w^\top \Sigma w = 1.
\]

Hence the optimal direction is
\[
\boxed{
w^\star = \frac{\Sigma^{-1} z}{\mathbf{1}^\top \Sigma^{-1} z}
}
\qquad \text{provided } \mathbf{1}^\top \Sigma^{-1} z \neq 0.
\]

This is the closed-form Sharpe-maximizing risky portfolio under the budget constraint.

With \(w^\star\) as above:
\[
\boxed{
\mu^\star = (w^\star)^\top \mu
= \frac{\mu^\top \Sigma^{-1} z}{\mathbf{1}^\top \Sigma^{-1} z}
}
\]

and
\[
\boxed{
(\sigma^\star)^2 = (w^\star)^\top \Sigma w^\star
= \frac{z^\top \Sigma^{-1} z}{(\mathbf{1}^\top \Sigma^{-1} z)^2}
}
\]

Let \(y\) be the fraction of wealth invested in the risky portfolio \(w^\star\)
and \(1-y\) invested in the risk-free asset. The total portfolio return is
\[
R_{\text{total}} = (1-y)r_f + y (w^\star)^\top R.
\]

Thus,
\[
\mathbb{E}[R_{\text{total}}] = r_f + y(\mu^\star - r_f),
\qquad
\mathrm{Var}(R_{\text{total}}) = y^2 (\sigma^\star)^2.
\]
